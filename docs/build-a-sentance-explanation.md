## Type 1: Asking Questions Back to the Prompt

A speaker presents a situation, and you are required to make a question by arranging the given words.
In this format, the sentence with blanks already includes a question mark, as shown below. This clearly indicates that you must create a question that fits the given prompt.

```
Prompt: The company decided to delay the product launch.

____ ____ ____ ____ ____ ____ ?

was / delay / reason / what / the / main
```

Because the question mark is already placed at the end of the sentence, you should focus on forming a grammatically correct question.

### Question 1:

```
Prompt: It seems that the conference schedule was revised at the last minute.

Were you ____ ____ ____ ____ ____ changes?

of / in / the / informed / advance
```

### Explanation:

When a question begins with a “to-be” verb such as “is” or “was,” it typically follows one of three patterns:

- Passive structure (to-be + subject + past participle)
- Progressive structure (to-be + subject + gerund)
- State-of-being structure (to-be + subject + adjective or noun phrase)

In the given word list, there are no adjectives or gerunds. This allows us to narrow the sentence type down to the passive structure, which follows the word order below.

Because this sentence is a passive question, the structure begins with “Were you,” followed by the past participle “informed.”

In the question, “changes” is the only noun. Since it refers to a specific set of changes already mentioned in the context, it requires the definite article “the,” forming “the changes.”

The expression “in advance of” is a prepositional phrase meaning before. Because “the changes” functions as the object of “of,” it must appear at the end of the sentence. As a result, the prepositional phrase naturally follows “informed.”

So, only one word order is grammatically possible:

> Were you informed in advance of the changes.

## Type 2: Responding to a Question

In Writing Part 1, you will be asked to do one of two things: either form a question or provide a response to a question. Recognizing which type of sentence is required is crucial.

If you need to form a question, the sentence must begin with a question-forming element, such as a to-be verb (e.g., is, was) or a 5W1H word (what, why, how, etc.).

Luckily, if the task requires a response rather than a question, the process is simpler. You can arrange the words using standard active-voice word order to create a grammatically correct statement.


### Explanation:

This sentence requires a relative clause that modifies the noun schedule. Remember that a relative clause is introduced by a relative pronoun and contains a complete clause (a subject and a verb). Its role is to add information about the noun that comes immediately before it.

by my supervisor is the agent phrase in passive voice, so it must follow was designed.

“that” must introduce the relative clause right after schedule.

The relative clause is passive: was designed (be + past participle).

> I relied on a schedule that was designed by my supervisor.

## Type 3: Beginning with Conjunction

Conjunctions are words used to connect clauses. In English, placing two independent clauses side by side without an appropriate connector results in an ungrammatical sentence. Consider the following examples:

> I like cats, but he likes dogs.
>
> *I like cats, he likes dogs.

The second sentence is ungrammatical because it incorrectly joins two independent clauses using only a comma. A comma by itself cannot connect independent clauses. This type of error is known as a comma splice, which is a common mistake in English writing. In contrast, the first sentence is grammatical because the coordinating conjunction “but” is used to properly link the two independent clauses.

Conjunctions can be broadly divided into two categories based on how they connect clauses.

- __Coordinating__: These conjunctions connect two independent clauses of equal grammatical status. They are commonly remembered using the acronym FANBOYS (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so).
- __Subordinating__: These conjunctions connect a dependent clause to an independent clause. A clause introduced by a subordinating conjunction cannot stand alone as a complete sentence. For TOEFL purposes, most conjunctions other than FANBOYS fall into this category.

### Example Sentences with Subordinating Conjunction

Case 1: Subordinating conjunction in the middle of the sentence

> I like dogs, while he likes cats.

In this sentence, “I like dogs” is an independent clause, and “while he likes cats” is a dependent clause introduced by the subordinating conjunction while. The two clauses are connected within a single sentence, and the comma separates the independent clause from the dependent clause.

Case 2: Subordinating conjunction at the beginning of the sentence

> While I like dogs, he likes cats.

Here, the subordinating conjunction appears at the sentence head. When this happens, the sentence must follow this structure:

> `Dependent clause + comma + independent clause`

In this example, “while I like dogs” is the dependent clause, and “he likes cats” is the independent clause. The comma is required to clearly separate the two clauses.

### Structure of Subordinating Conjunction-opening Sentences

> `Dependent clause + comma + independent clause`
>
> Here, a dependent clause refers to a clause that begins with a subordinating conjunction such as although, while, because, or if.
> An independent clause is a complete sentence that can stand on its own.
>
> <Example>
> Although I feel bad today, I have to go to school to take an important exam.

Understanding this structure is especially important for TOEFL Writing Section Part 1, where you must determine the correct word order from a list of words. When a sentence clearly begins with a subordinating conjunction, you can immediately conclude that:

- the conjunction must be followed by a dependent clause, and
- a comma must separate it from the independent clause that follows.

This rule is consistent and exception-free, which makes it a reliable anchor when solving sentence-construction questions.

   First, confirm which word in the list functions as a subordinating conjunction and can appear at the beginning of the sentence.
2. Find the subject:
   Next, locate the noun or noun phrase that can function as the subject of the dependent clause.
3. Identify the verb:
   Determine which word or phrase serves as the verb, paying attention to voice (active or passive) and tense.
4. Arrange the remaining words meaningfully:
   Use the remaining words in the word bank to complete the clause in a way that is both grammatical and semantically natural.
5. Combine everything into a full sentence:
   Remember that when a sentence starts with a subordinating conjunction, the structure is fixed: dependent clause + comma + independent clause.

Because this structure is predictable, you can solve these questions logically rather than relying on intuition. Now, let’s practice applying these steps to master this question pattern.

Next, we identify the verb. The word list contains only one verb form: “were resolved,” which is a passive construction. This fits naturally with the subject “concerns,” since concerns are resolved by someone, not acting on their own.

Putting these parts together, we get a grammatically complete dependent clause. The independent clause then follows, separated by a comma, as required for sentence-initial subordinating conjunctions.

> Until all outstanding concerns were resolved, she postponed the decision.

## Type 4: A Sentence with Relative Pronoun

Relative pronouns are words used to add information about a noun or noun phrase that appears earlier in a sentence. They introduce relative clauses, which help specify or explain the noun being modified.

Remember that a clause is a group of words that contains both a subject and a verb. A relative clause, therefore, always includes these two elements and functions to give additional detail about the noun it follows.
Six Common Relative Pronouns

The most commonly tested relative pronouns on the TOEFL are the following:

- Who
- Whom
- Whose
- Which
- That
- What

However, note that “what” behaves differently from the others. While the first five modify an explicit noun, “what” functions as a fused (free) relative pronoun. It includes the noun it refers to and does not modify a preceding noun.

Choosing the correct relative pronoun depends on:

- whether the noun refers to a person or a thing
- whether the pronoun functions as a subject or object
- whether possession is involved

The table below summarizes the key differences among these relative pronouns.


| Relative Pronoun | Refers to | Grammatical Role | Can Modify a Preceding Noun? | Example |
|---|---|---:|:---:|---|
| who | People | Subject | Yes | The student who asked the question was confused. |
| whom | People | Object | Yes | The professor whom we met was helpful. |
| whose | People / Things | Possessive | Yes | The researcher whose theory was rejected revised it. |
| which | Things / ideas | Subject or object | Yes | The policy which was introduced last year failed. |
| that | People / Things | Subject or object | Yes | The rule that applies here is strict. |
| what | Things (implicit noun) | Subject or object | No | I understood what he meant. |


### Question 1

```
Prompt: Which candidate did the committee reject?

The committee ____ ____ ____ ____ ____ ____ ____ ____ ____.

who / requirements / a / minimum / failed / candidate / to / meet / rejected
```

### Explanation

Let’s start by identifying the core clause. Among options, you may find that “The committee rejected a candidate” is a complete independent clause.

- The committee: subject
- rejected: verb
- a candidate: object

The remaining words must explain which candidate was rejected.

Since the word bank includes “who,” we know that a relative clause modifying a person is required. Among the words available, “a candidate” is the only noun that refers to a person, so it must be the word being modified.

A relative pronoun is placed immediately after the noun it modifies and must be followed by a clause containing a subject and a verb. Using this rule, we can construct the following sentence:

> The committee rejected a candidate who failed to meet minimum requirements.

Here, “who” refers back to “a candidate.” The relative clause begins with the verb “failed,” and the rest of the clause explains what the candidate failed to do.

## Type 5: Simple Past vs Past Perfect

One common use of the past perfect tense is to clarify the order of two past events within the same sentence. In written English, when two past actions are mentioned, the event that happened earlier is often expressed in the past perfect so that the time relationship is clear.

Let’s see an example of this grammar.

> I was shocked by the fact that I had lost my wallet.

This sentence refers to two different events. First, the speaker lost their wallet. Later, they realized what had happened and felt shocked. Because the loss occurred before the emotional reaction, “had lost” is written in the past perfect. This tense signals that the wallet was already lost at the moment the speaker felt shocked.

Note that in everyday conversation, however, speakers don’t always have to make this distinction.

### Question 1

```
Prompt: Why did the historian revise the interpretation?

The conclusion changed _____ _____ _____ _____ _____ _____.

had / sources / primary / because / emerged / new
```

### Explanation

The first step is to create a meaningful combination of a subject and a verb that forms a clause. Keep in mind that only a noun, a noun phrase, or a gerund can function as the subject of a sentence.

In the word bank, the only noun available is “sources,” which naturally works with the verb “emerged.” This gives us the basic clause:

> sources emerged

There are also two adjectives, “new” and “primary.” Both modify the noun “sources” and are placed before it in the order “new primary.“ As a result, the clause becomes as follows:

> new primary sources emerged

Next, consider the time relationship between the events mentioned in the sentence. Here, two events are involved:

- The conclusion changed
- new primary sources emerged

The key question is which event happened first.

In this case, it is logical to think that the new sources appear first, and the conclusion is revised afterward. This order makes sense because conclusions are typically updated in response to new evidence. If we reverse the order and say that the sources emerged after the conclusion had already changed, the situation no longer sounds realistic.

Because the discovery of the new sources occurred earlier, it should be expressed using the past perfect tense. This gives us the clause:

> new primary sources had emerged.

At this point, we have two complete clauses:

- The conclusion changed
- New primary sources had emerged

To connect these two clauses, we need a conjunction. In the word bank, the only available option is “because,” so we use it to show the cause-and-effect relationship. The completed sentence is:

> The conclusion changed because new primary sources had emerged.

## Type 6: Cracking the Participial Construction

A participial construction is an advanced grammatical structure in English that allows you to add additional information to a sentence without using a conjunction. This structure is commonly used to make sentences more concise and fluid.

### Basic Structure of a Participial Construction

> Sentences with a participial construction typically follow one of the patterns below:
>
> - Complete sentence + comma + *Gerund phrase
> - *Gerund phrase + comma + Complete sentence
>
> 【Important!!】
> The participial constructions work only when the implied subject of the gerund phrase matches the subject of the main clause. If the subjects do not match, the sentence becomes unclear or misleading.
>
> *A gerund phrase is any phrase that begins with a “verb-ing” (present participle) form, such as “swimming,” “running,” or “believing.”

### Example Sentences with Participial Construction

Below is a list of sentences that have a participial construction.

> Swimming towards the shore, I saw a girl making a sand castle.
>
> The teacher participated in many teaching seminars, believing that the experience would improve his teaching quality.

Let’s examine them one by one.
First Example

> Swimming towards the shore, I saw a girl making a sand castle.

This sentence is grammatical because it follows the “gerund phrase + comma + complete sentence” structure. As explained earlier, a gerund phrase is a phrase that begins with a “verb-ing” (present participle) form.

In this example, “Swimming towards the shore” functions as the gerund phrase. It’s a phrase, not a sentence, because it doesn’t contain a main verb. This phrase is followed by the complete sentence “I saw a girl making a sand castle.”

The crucial point here is subject consistency. The implied subject of the gerund phrase is “I,” meaning that I am the one who is swimming towards the shore. Therefore, the subject of the main clause that follows must also be “I.” If a different noun is placed after the gerund phrase, the sentence becomes unclear or misleading.

For example, the following sentence is incorrect:

> Swimming towards the shore, a girl was making a sand castle.

This sentence incorrectly suggests that the girl was swimming towards the shore while making a sand castle, which is impossible, unless she has more than two arms. The mismatch between the implied subject of the gerund phrase and the subject of the main clause makes the sentence unacceptable.
Second Example

> The teacher participated in many teaching seminars, believing that the experience would improve his teaching quality.

This is a longer sentence, but the underlying grammatical principle is the same as in the previous example.

First, the sentence is grammatical because it follows the “complete sentence + comma + gerund phrase” structure. Unlike the first example, the complete sentence comes first, followed by the gerund phrase.

Second, the implied subject of the gerund phrase correctly matches the subject of the main clause, “the teacher.” The sentence as a whole conveys that the teacher participated in many seminars because he believed that the experience would improve his teaching quality.

Because the implied subject and the main subject are aligned, the participial construction works smoothly and adds extra information without causing ambiguity.

### Question 2

```
Prompt: Why did the committee reject the proposal?

_____ _____ _____ _____ _____ _____, the committee had rejected the proposal.

claims / examining / the / evidence / to / related
```

### Explanation

Note that when combining two complete sentences, you must place a conjunction between them to make the sentence grammatical as a whole. Compare the following two sentences, one grammatical and the other ungrammatical:

> I am studying for TOEFL, and it’s going great.
>
> I am studying for TOEFL, it’s going great.

According to formal grammar rules, only the first sentence is grammatical. The second sentence is ungrammatical because two complete sentences are connected by a single comma, which does not have the function of joining independent clauses. This type of error is commonly called a “comma splice” or a “run-on error.”

Now, look at the given sentence in the question. Two parts are connected by a comma, but there is no conjunction between them. This means that what comes before the comma is not a complete sentence. Instead, it must be either a dependent structure or a specific grammatical construction.
